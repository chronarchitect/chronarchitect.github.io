Philosophy // 002

Is it all grind, or is there light at the end of the tunnel?

On burning out quietly inside a machine that keeps moving the goalposts

I am 27 years old, and I quietly quit my office. Not my job — just the office. There is no work-from-home policy at my firm, but I work from home anyway, because I refuse to spend two hours of my life every single day grinding through the streets of Bengaluru. That is two hours I did not consent to losing. So I just... stopped.

The desk job pays the bills. More than my dad ever made. Enough to support a family of three, theoretically. And yet here I am, writing this instead of working, asking myself when exactly I signed up for this.

The honest answer is: I did not sign up. I escaped. There is a difference, and it matters. The job was never a dream — it was an exit. The city was never a choice — it was where the opportunity landed. When your first real goal in life is simply to earn enough to make your own decisions, you do not get to be picky about what you are escaping into. You just run, and figure out the rest later. I am still figuring out the rest.

···

The corporate machine, to its credit, is efficient at exactly one thing: making you feel like the problem. Moving goalposts get rebranded as ambition. Efficiency gains go unacknowledged, but inefficiency gets a meeting. The latest trick is citing "AI productivity" to raise expectations, while simultaneously capping the AI token budget so you cannot actually use the models that are worth a damn — you get to do more, faster, with the slop tier. The math does not work. But you are not supposed to do the math. You are supposed to be grateful.

No appreciation for efficiency gains, but reprimands when not. That is the whole game, really — and everyone knows it, and nobody says it out loud.

The scrum team is genuinely good. Cohesive, low-drama, the kind of tight-knit environment where upper management bullshit has not fully penetrated the veil yet. I hold onto that. But the institution above keeps doing institution things. The return-to-office mandate arrived like a hanging sword over everyone's necks — technically a policy, functionally a layoff threat with a delay timer on it. An answer is expected. I have not given one.

One morning — 11am, because I had been up all night asking myself what the hell I am doing with my life — I was standing on the balcony, groggy, staring at nothing, and I caught myself thinking: getting laid off would actually be great. At least then someone else makes the call. At least I stop doing this exhausting half-in, half-out thing where I want to make things and live at my own pace and wake up when I want — while also showing up to standups and pretending I care about sprint velocity.

And the truly fun part? The thought of getting laid off causes its own work anxiety. Both feelings exist simultaneously. Burnt out and scared of losing the thing burning you out. I have made my bed, and I am lying in it, and I am not willing to destroy my mind and body answering every beck and call of the corporate machine. Not commuting probably has consequences for my career. I think I am okay with that. Or I think I think I am okay with that.

[ break — the gummies hit — in the middle of a meeting, of all places — a trip, some music, a nap, and an espresso later ]

I live in the silicon valley of India. The "of India" part is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence. Every day this city reminds me of why that qualifier exists — roads that demand off-road vehicles, flooding after light rain, tier 1 on paper and Temu tier in practice. When you order a tier 1 city and it arrives slightly damaged with the packaging half open. I do not say this with hatred. I say it with the flat, tired energy of someone who never chose to be here and has been here long enough to stop pretending it does not matter.

The job bought me freedom once. That part is real and I am not trying to be ungrateful about it — the version of me who could not buy a phone without a guilt trip would not believe the life I have now. But there is a point where the golden handcuffs stop feeling like a prize and start feeling like just — handcuffs. Technically free. Technically not. Pick one.

···

The manifestation reels on my feed say assume it is already yours and it will come to you. I have been assuming time freedom for years. The remote job market is genuinely bad, applications largely disappear into the void, and the universe, as far as I can tell, remains an eternally silent void that does not much care what you assume.

What I do have: evenings. Nights, really. 3am is when I feel most like myself, which probably says something I am not ready to fully examine yet. I have been building things — IoT projects, a YouTube video with my actual face and name on it (a first, and a bigger deal than it sounds), a canvas painting that is now on my wall. Small things. Real things. Mine.

There has got to be more to life than this. I know it the way you know something you cannot prove. A level of financial independence where I am not trading my time for a corporate salary, living a life I actually chose rather than one I escaped into. I am not there yet. I am not close. But I am done pretending the current arrangement is fine, and I am done being quiet about it.

Written at 3am. Espresso-assisted. Gummy-interrupted. First draft, honest draft.
